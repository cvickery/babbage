Audio Encoding

Laboratory Exercises

There are two programs available for you to use to explore how audio signals are digitized:
  • Audio Sampling
  • Fourier Synthesis

The audio sampling program is designed to help you visualize the effects of sampling rate and bits per sample when an analog signal, on the left, is digitized to produce the waveform displayed on the right.

The Fourier Synthesis program is designed to help you visualize how complex sound waves can be generated by mixing a small number of sine waves. If you are familiar with audio equipment, an equalizer is a device that varies the amplitudes of the frequency components of sounds.

The first program deals with the concepts covered in class on September 13; the second one is extra material intended to get beyond simple sine waves. Try them both before class if you can; weâ€™ll go over them both in class on the assignment due date.

Written Exercises

  1. The frequency or rate with which something happens can be measured n Hertz (abbreviated Hz). Something that happens one time per second has a frequency of one Hertz (1 Hz). Something that happens two times per second has a frequency of 2 Hz. What is the frequency of something that happens 10 times per second? Your answer must specify both the number and the unit of measure (Hz).
  2. If audio sampling is done 100 times per second, what is the sampling rate?
  3. K is the abbreviation for 1,000. You can combine it with a rate measure to get KHz. How many samples per second is 44.1 KHz? Write out the complete number.
  4. The sampling rate for CD-quality audio is 44.1 KHz, and for stereo, there are two audio channels. How many samples will be taken when digitizing CD-quality audio for 10 seconds in stereo?
  5. If each audio sample is saved as a 16-bit number, how many bits will be needed to store to store 10 seconds of CD-quality audio in stereo?
  6. 1 M = 1000 K. How many bits are needed in order to store one hour of CD-quality audio? Answer in Mb ("megabits").
