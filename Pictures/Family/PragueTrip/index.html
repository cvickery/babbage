July 10, 2002 - NYC to London to Prague

We were supposed to leave NYC at 6:00 PM on July 10th, but our flight waited on the runway at JFK for a couple of hours waiting for thunderstorms to pass. And for Air Force 1, which we later learned had tied up traffic for a bit while President Bush exhorted the business community to do as he says. The net result was that we missed our connecting flight from Heathrow to Prague, and had several hours to kill waiting for the next one out. Since it's a "quick, easy trip" from the airport to London via Metro, we decided to hop on in to while away the time. Well, we waited in the Metro station for the better part of an hour waiting for a train to arrive, but that still left us some time to explore the area around Picadilly Circus. We decided not to buy one of the $250 hairbrushes this time, but did pick up some treats at Fortnum & Mason.

We got back to Heathrow in plenty of time for our plane, despite the Metro station at Picadilly being closed because of a suspicious package. Not that it mattered much. The plane sat in the rain for an hour before we took off. Finally, after 23 hours of travel time, we made it to our apartment in in the Malá Strana (Lesser Town) section of Prague. Went out for a quick walk over Karlův Most (Charles Bridge). This is the 14th century bridge lined with religious statues that spans the Vltava (Moldau). Had dinner at a Czech-only place with Kolya. Dinner for three with 2.5 liters of beer totalled $12.


The view from our window, taken just before we started our jet lag recovery process.

July 11 - Sightseeing in Prague

We did a short walk in the neighborhood on our own, then Kolya took us on a good "overview" walk of Staré Město (Old Town) and Josefov (Jewish Quarter). We had dinner with Kolya at a place known to have a "clean tap" and good Czech food. He was right on both counts.

July 12 - Prague

Woke up to the sound of squealing swallows, a sound that continued to be characteristic of Prague.

Walked up to the castle and down through the center of Prague to look for a milk frother. (We found one, but at $50 we decided a fork worked just fine). Stopped for lunch by the water just before crossing the Charles Bridge. Then walked on through Old Town to pick up the rental car at Avis, a blue Opel. Drove back through town, got lost looking for Kolya's apartment, and helped a French woman locate her hotel while phoning Kolya for directions to his place. Parked fairly near our apartment for the night, a spot that proved to be both convenient and secure from car thieves throughout our stay.

Had dinner at Kogo, an Italian restaurant recommended by Kolya. Food was very good: risotto of mushrooms, veal scaloppini. Dessert back at the apartment: the mousse we had bought at Fortnum & Mason.

July 13 - Bušhtěhrad, Lidice, Karlovy Vary, Mariánské Lázně

Kolya prepared breakfast for us at his place, after which we drove to Bušhtěhrad for the flea market.

Across the road from the turnoff for Bušhtěhrad, is the memorial to the Nazi victims of Lidice.

We drove on from Ludice to Karlovy Vary (Carlsbad) to take the waters, stopping for lunch when we spotted a barbeque by the side of the road.

It was the last day of the international film festival at Karlovy Vary, and President Václav Havel was expected to attend.


Waiting for Havel

We drove on from Karlovy Vary to Mariánské Lázně (Marienbad), where we had rooms at a The Koliba, a rustic lodge on the edge of town. We went for a short walk in the rain, and found our car being booted when we returned. It turns out that little Mariánské Lázně has the third highest car-theft rate in the Czech Republic, and booting is a courtesy the hotel provides for its guests.


What looks like Queen Anne's Lace is a totally different species of plant, about six feet tall with blossoms larger than a dinner plate.

July 14 - Mariánské Lázně, Plzeň, and back to Prague

We walked through Mariánské Lázně, sampled the waters, watched the crowd line up as the Kolonáda Oplatky shop opened. Oplatky are "spa wafers" that are best when heated in a press just before eating. Guess they go well with the waters.

From Mariánské Lázně we drove to Plzeň, the home of Pilsner Urquell.


Poppies on the road to Plzeň

On the way to Plzeň, we stopped at Kladruby where there is a Benedictine monastery that Kolya was particularly hoping to see. There were no tours until 1:00 and we had to be in Plzeň in time for the last brewery tour at 2:00, so we had to be content looking around the grounds.

After touring the brewery, visiting the beer museum, and cooling off at the town square, we drove back to Kolya's apartment in Prague, where he prepared our dinner, and we spent the night.


No bigger than a thumbnail.

July 15 - Prague to Český Krumlov

I got back from a morning jog in time to say good-bye to Kolya as he left for work. We packed our things for Austria, and took off mid-day. We drove to Český Krumlov, which is a beautiful place to visit, but would have been better if the crowds hadn't been so thick. Driving through the throngs of tourists clogging the main street of the old town part of the city as we arrived made neither the crowds nor us particularly comfortable. The restaurant recommended by Frommer's was at the top of a 1,000 foot climb above the castle ... and closed Mondays. Trout in another restaurant in the center of town was wonderful, and no crowd to speak of. Kručovice, the beer Kolya introduced us to in Prague, remains a fine choice.


Three weeks later

July 16 - Český Krumlov, Bad Ischl, Wolfgansee

Our room in Český Krumlov was so nice we debated staying on for another day, but the rain and busloads of tourists convinced us to move on. The last few miles of the drive into Austria were populated with prostitutes by the woods. The scenery changed as soon as we crossed the border.


"Like being in another country."

We drove to St. Wolfgang on the shore of Wolfgangsee, where we managed to snag a room at Landhaus zu Appesbach, famous because the Duke of Windsor loved it so much that he stayed there for three months in 1939. We stretched our stay as long as we could, staying in three different rooms the four nights we were there. The meals (dinner, breakfast, and afternoon tea) were superb, the staff was wonderful, and the scenery outstanding.

July 17 - Bad Ischl and the Salzkammergut

The weather was rainy as could be. We viewed rock carvings on the wall of an elementary school, went for a walk by Schwartzensee, and drove on to Bad Ischl, where Chris was born. We found the hospital (Krankenhaus) where she was born, the church where she was christened (St. Nikolaus), and neighborhood where she lived (Perneck).

It was hot, steep, and Chris was not a happy camper for the last hour or so of the hike despite the fine views and wildflowers.

July 20 - Last day in Austria

After checking out, we walked into town for some shopping, came back, and enjoyed the lake some more before taking off for Prague late in the afternoon. The prostitutes at the border seemed to be much more professional on this Saturday evening than the ones we had seen Tuesday afternoon on our way down.

Ebensee marked the end of the Alps as we headed north towards Linz and the Czech border.

July 21 - Prague

I got up early for a Sunday morning walk across Charles Bridge and around the mostly-deserted Old Town, spoiled somewhat by the drunks still carousing in the Old Town square. A number of places put up "No football teams or bachelor parties" signs because the guys get too rowdy.


The place where we had dinner twice with Kolya

"Did" Tesco, including the supermarket in the basement, returned the car to Avis, and walked back to meet Kolya at the apartment. It's such a pleasure to have him with us when we walk around; so many wonderful tidbits of information, for example.

July 23 - Tour of Prague Castle, St. Vitus, etc.

We took the tram to the Prague Castle area, bought our admission tickets, and did everything ... got all the holes punched in our tickets to prove it. Well, I only made it up 38 of the 287 steps of the steeple of St. Vitus before turning back; too crowded for my claustrophobia. But other than that, we did it all.


Three Kings blessing inside St. Vitus. (see below)

"On the evening before Three Kings [January 6], ... doorways would be sprinkled with holy water and the master of the house would write with chalk C + M + B and the year above the house and barn door and say: "Caspar, Melchior, Balthasar, behütet uns auch für dieses Jahr, vor Feuer und vor Wassergefahr." ("CMB, protect us again this year from the dangers of fire and water.") C + M + B has traditionally been translated with Caspar, Melchior and Balthasar, however, according to the Church it stands for 'Christus Mansionem Benedictat' (Christ bless this home)." [http://www.serve.com/shea/germusa/3kings.htm]. The plus signs are crosses.
