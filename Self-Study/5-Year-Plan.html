Future of Department for Next Five to Seven Years

Describe and analyze, from the point of view of the department’s faculty, the department's strengths and weaknesses, proposed changes, and other areas of concern. Special attention should be given to issues regarding faculty recruitment and retention, especially with regard to women and minorities, as recruitment often plays a vital role in the future of the department, to the extent that it is usually more convenient to first discuss the five-to-seven-year future of the department and the specific actions, particularly the recruitment of new faculty, needed to achieve that future. Note that a clear formulation of the department’s mission statement in Section 1 will greatly facilitate the discussion here of the department’s future.

As “the” technology department at the college, Computer Science attracts many students who pursue their undergraduate education with a quite limited, career-oriented objective: to prepare themselves for their first job. This is an unfortunate situation because it does not match the department’s own mission, which is to integrate today's digital technologies into a well-rounded liberal arts education. Nonetheless, the situation exists, and it leads to rather large cyclical fluctuations in our enrollments as the perceived employability of computer scientists fluctuates with socioeconomic conditions affecting the technology sector of the economy. At the present time, we are at a low point in the number of students majoring or minoring in our department following the dot-com bubble burst of 2002 and subsequent concerns about off-shoring of programming jobs. Thus, our plans for the next few years must be made in this context, and need to address its implications. What can we do to stabilize our cyclical enrollment patterns? What can we do to prepare for the next wave? And most importantly, what can we do to provide better integration of our curriculum with the needs of Queens College students, not just Computer Science majors, in an era where a solid mastery of technological issues and trends is expected of all members of society?

Stabilizing Enrollment

Historically (if one can speak historically of the youngest department in the Natural Sciences!), computer science has been a “big major,” with around sixty credits of required courses, and a “big department,” with the fourth largest number of majors at the college in 2003, the most recent year for which comparative data are available. However from 2001 to 2004 our FTEs dropped by close to 40%. But we know that technological skills are a critical element in the educational fabric of our times, and we have every reason to believe that our current enrollments are the low point in a cyclic process.

There are three fronts for dealing with the issue of enrollment cycles: curriculum, staffing, and (for lack of a better term) marketing.

  1. Curriculum

The computer science major is objectively large, and is generally perceived to be difficult. It is large because of the complexity of the subject matter, and difficult because mastery tends be be bimodal: either you work hard and “get it,” or you might as well not bother to try. The minor is a proper subset of the major, so it too is not something that can be pursued casually.

The content and structure of the major is highly structured and largely dictated by norms developed by professional societies in the field and other academic institutions. So, except for the normal process of refinement and evolution, the major is not going to change much for the next five to seven years. However, the department also offers several courses specifically designed for non-majors, and we see programs for non-majors not only as a way of dampening the fluctuations in our FTEs, but also as a way of recruiting well-qualified students to pursue the major.

One popular set of courses for non-majors can be thought of as basic skills courses: the use of office skills like word processing, spreadsheets, and simple web page design. We use our understanding of computer science principles to infuse these courses with sound principles of algorithmic thinking and development of realistic technological models as much as we can. As students arrive at the college with better and better technological backgrounds, these course evolve to include more advanced topics, but by their nature they will never become “real” computer science courses because they do not require the mathematical background of even the most elementary courses in the computer science major and minor. Nonetheless, keeping these courses up to date and relevant to the “real world” provides us with a mechanism for attracting students to the department.

An possible option would be to expand our curriculum into other computer-related disciplines mentioned in the introduction to this report: Computer Engineering, Information Technology, Software Engineering, or Information Systems. Perhaps by averaging our enrollment across several majors we could stablize the total number of students we teach. We feel this would be a risky approach requiring changes that are too radical for the department to undertake. While our present curriculum overlaps with several of these other disciplines (notably software engineering and computer engineering), but we would require many more faculty lines to be able to offer full majors in any of these other areas, and we would still be faced with cyclic demand among the majors, leading to inevitable imbalances between the areas we cover and the interests of the students. Furthermore, we feel strongly that computer science is the most appropriate program for providing technology-centric education in a liberal arts college such as Queens.

However, we do see courses for non-majors as an attractive vehicle for stabilizing our enrollment base while attracting good students into the major. To this end, we see specialized minors as a way of expanding this approach. For example, we are experimenting with a (“web services”?) minor that will provide instruction in the design and deployment of internet-based applications. While courses in this minor could be taught from an Information Technology perspective stressing the use of commercial tools, our courses will stress design principles and realistic models based on algorithmic problem-solving. We are hopeful that this minor will be seen by students as relevant and stimulating while also motivating some of them to dig deeper by taking courses in the major.

  1. Staffing

An unfortunate side effect of cyclical enrollment patterns is that the number of full-time faculty tends to lag the number needed to meet student demands. Some of this lag can be handled by having a relatively small number of full-time faculty and a fluctuating body of adjuncts to fill the slack as demand waxes and wanes. While we are fortunate to have several excellent adjunct faculty members, relying on adjuncts in general leads to serious problems of quality control and management.

For the next one or two years, we would like to maintain approximately the current number of full-time faculty. There should be new hires during this period because of normal attrition from the current ranks of the department, and this is a time for us to be sure we continue to have a good balance of people whose interests cover the core areas of computer science: software, hardware, and theory.
